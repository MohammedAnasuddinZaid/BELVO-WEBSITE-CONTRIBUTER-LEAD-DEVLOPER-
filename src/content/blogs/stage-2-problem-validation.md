You have an idea. Nice. Now comes the less glamorous part: finding out whether anyone else cares.

Problem validation is where founders stop guessing and start listening. The aim is not to collect compliments. The aim is to discover whether a real group of people has a real problem, feels it often enough, and would actually switch behaviour to solve it.

## Talk to People Who Already Feel the Pain

Start with people who match the audience from stage one. Do not pitch them your product immediately. Ask about the last time the problem happened.

Questions like these get better answers than "Would you use this?":

- When did this last happen?
- What did you do instead?
- How much time, money, or stress did it create?
- What have you already tried?
- What would make you change your current way of doing it?

Specific stories are gold. Vague encouragement is not. If someone cannot remember a recent example, the problem may not be urgent enough yet.

## Look for Behaviour, Not Polite Yeses

People are kind. They will tell you an idea is "cool" even when they have no intention of using it. That is why behaviour matters more than praise.

Better signals include someone introducing you to another person with the same problem, joining a waitlist, agreeing to test a prototype, sharing internal data, or paying for a manual version. These actions cost effort. Effort is a much stronger signal than a thumbs-up.

## Find the Workaround You Are Competing With

Your competitor may not be a big company with a shiny website. It could be a spreadsheet, a template, an intern, a group chat, or a process people dislike but tolerate.

Ask people to show you their current workaround. That shows you where time is being lost and where trust breaks down. It also stops you from building a feature that solves a problem nobody actually has.

## Test the Riskiest Assumption First

Every startup idea has assumptions hiding underneath it. Will people trust us? Will they pay? Can we reach them? Is the workflow painful enough to replace?

Write those assumptions down, then test the one that could break the whole idea. Do not spend three months perfecting the design before answering the question that matters most.

A quick landing page, interview script, prototype, or concierge-style trial can teach you more than a polished but untested product.

## Turn What You Hear Into a Sharper Position

Validation should change your wording. If customers describe the problem differently from you, use their language. If one audience segment reacts much more strongly, focus on them first. If the pain is not strong enough, adjust the idea before you invest more.

## Do Not Be Afraid of a No

Learning that an idea needs to change is progress, not failure. A quick no is cheaper than a slow launch nobody notices.

The goal of validation is confidence earned through evidence. Keep talking, keep testing, and let the real problem lead the product.

**If people will not change their behaviour, your solution still has homework to do.**
