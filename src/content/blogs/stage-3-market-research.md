You have found a problem people recognise. Great. Now you need to understand the room you are walking into.

Market research is not about becoming an expert on every company in your industry. It is about getting clear on the people, alternatives, pricing, language, and gaps that will shape your next move.

## Define the Market in Human Terms

Start with the people, not a giant market-size number. Who are they? What job are they trying to get done? What stage are they at? What do they care about when they choose a solution?

You might segment by job role, business size, location, industry, budget, behaviour, or level of urgency. The useful segment is the one that helps you make a better decision about product, message, or channel.

If your audience description could fit almost anyone, make it narrower. Specificity is not limiting. It is useful.

## Study Alternatives Like a Customer Would

Search the way your customer searches. Visit competitor websites, read reviews, compare pricing pages, and pay attention to what people praise or complain about.

Look at direct competitors, but also look at indirect alternatives. A founder might choose another tool, hire an agency, use a template, or keep doing the work manually. All of those options compete for the same time and budget.

Build a simple comparison table for yourself: audience, promise, pricing approach, proof, weak points, and customer language. You are not copying anyone. You are looking for the open space.

## Find a Gap That Actually Matters

Not every difference is meaningful. A different gradient colour is not positioning. A faster onboarding flow, clearer reporting, trusted local support, a better niche focus, or a simpler pricing model might be.

The best gap sits at the intersection of three things: something customers value, something competitors do poorly, and something your team can deliver consistently.

That is the beginning of a real position in the market.

## Validate Price and Demand Early

Pricing is part of research, not a final detail. Find out what people pay for existing options, what they believe the problem costs them, and what result would make a solution feel worth it.

Use public data for broad trends, then speak directly to your target audience for the details that only they can tell you. Market reports can show the landscape. Customer conversations tell you whether your idea has a place in it.

## Turn Research Into a Launch Plan

Research only matters when it changes your actions. Use it to decide:

- Which customer group you will serve first
- What message will make them pay attention
- Which proof points they need before trusting you
- Where you can reach them
- What your first offer should include

BELVO helps founders turn this information into a brand that makes sense in the real market - not just on a mood board. From positioning to website messaging, the goal is to make your difference easy to understand and hard to ignore.

## Make the Next Move With Context

You do not need perfect research. You need enough evidence to make a smarter next decision than you could have made yesterday.

**Research is not a delay before the launch. It is how you stop launching blind.**
